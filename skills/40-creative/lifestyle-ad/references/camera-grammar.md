# Camera Grammar

Use this reference when designing the look and shot logic of a State-Led Lifestyle Ad. These are grammar choices, not mandatory shot order.

## Core Idea

The ad makes a product capability visible through a desirable human state: calm, taste, precision, ease, confidence, presence, speed, warmth, or control.

## Grammar Principles

### Body Before Product

Introduce the product through a body relationship: worn, carried, held, leaned on, touched, opened, placed, or used in rhythm. The viewer should read the person before reading the feature.

Good signals:

- wearable already on the body
- bag already on the shoulder
- cup already in hand
- vehicle already moving through the user's day
- digital or physical result used as a quiet proof object after the moment

### Close-Up As Status Anchor

Use close-ups to establish state, not to explain. Favor selected details: side profile, eyes, hands, shoulder line, product edge, fabric, cup, desk, door, wheel, handle, light on material.

The close-up should answer: "What kind of person is this product making visible?"

### Stable Subject / Moving World

Create premium tension by keeping the subject composed while the world moves around them.

Useful motion contrast:

- people pass through foreground
- documents slide across a table
- doors open and close
- traffic, hallway movement, classroom movement, restaurant motion, airport flow
- hands gesture around a still, focused subject

### Pressure Carried By Environment

Avoid overacting panic. Let objects and surroundings carry pressure.

Pressure carriers:

- pen pausing above notes
- laptop taps
- quick hand gestures
- cup set down
- page turn
- suitcase wheel
- receipt, calendar, badge, ticket, menu, contract, notebook
- off-camera line that changes the stakes

### Product Gesture As Ordinary Life Action

The product action should look like something the person would naturally do anyway.

Examples:

- adjust a worn item
- tap a hinge or side once
- lift a cup
- open a case
- place a phone down
- close a laptop
- pull a handle
- glance at a result

Do not make the gesture feel like a technical ritual unless the brand requires technical drama.

### Proof As A Glimpse

Proof appears briefly and specifically. It should confirm the mechanism without becoming a tutorial.

Proof can be:

- a phone check
- a result card
- a saved note
- a finished action
- a calmer next step
- another person's relaxed reaction
- a visible object now handled smoothly

When generated text is unreliable, carry proof through action, expression, and simple interface structure rather than dense readable UI.

### Observational Framing

Frame the scene like the camera is observing a life in progress, not pitching a feature.

Prefer:

- side profile
- three-quarter profile
- over-shoulder context
- low table-level insert
- hand-detail insert
- foreground wipe
- walking or doorway transition

Use direct-to-camera only when creator trust is the point.

### Sound As World Texture

Use sound to make the scene inhabited:

- room tone
- footsteps
- page turn
- cup set down
- pen pause
- door latch
- traffic wash
- chair shift
- off-camera line

Keep voice short when the ad is state-led. Let the world and reaction carry part of the message.

### Sparse Text As Judgment

Text should name a state, judgment, or proof beat. It should not become a feature list.

Better text jobs:

- "Got it."
- "No phone out."
- "Already packed."
- "Still present."
- "Every detail, saved."

Avoid turning text into a product brochure unless the user explicitly wants performance ads.

### Identity Close

The ending should name the user's way of being or doing.

It can take many forms:

- "Made for days that move fast."
- "For people who do not miss the detail."
- "Quietly ready."
- "The day keeps moving. So does she."
- "Still present, even when the day speeds up."

Do not require any one phrase. Match the brand voice.
