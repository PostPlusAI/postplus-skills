# Educational Grammar

Use this reference when designing the argument, camera, evidence, and offer logic.

## Core Arc

Build the recurring human movement:

`recognition of pressure -> concern, skepticism, or curiosity -> named reason -> visible evidence -> practical agency`

Education must reduce ambiguity. Do not merely add facts to a product pitch.

## Education Engines

Choose one primary engine:

- `counterintuitive mechanism`: challenge the assumed cause and explain another supported cause
- `ranked comparison`: compare options through stable criteria before revealing the preferred response
- `question and answer`: begin with a real audience question and answer it through evidence
- `consequence narrative`: show a person, team, or system living through the cost of missing knowledge
- `objection resolution`: state skepticism, inspect the offer, then answer with demonstration or proof
- `expert explainer`: use a qualified anchor to connect problem, reason, consequence, and response
- `interview demonstration`: let a user describe pressure, handle the offer, and react to observable behavior
- `visual mechanism`: use animation, process imagery, or object behavior to make an invisible process legible

Do not mix several engines unless one clearly remains primary.

## Anchor

Use one stable source of narrative continuity:

- direct-to-camera educator
- expert or operator in a role-relevant setting
- interviewer and participant
- narrator over a recurring protagonist
- animated character or system
- comparison interface or tier structure

The anchor must be credible from supplied facts and behavior. Wardrobe and set dressing cannot manufacture unsupported credentials.

## Evidence System

Choose a coherent system and repeat it:

- clause-matched real-world B-roll
- physical demonstration or material inspection
- approved comparison criteria
- process, ingredient, label, or interface inspection
- before/after behavior or state
- audience, customer, or operator reaction
- documented result, test, guarantee, or policy
- narrative counterfactual showing the cost of inaction

Use at least two compatible proof modes when evidence supports them. Aspirational imagery is not proof.

## Camera And Framing

- Use frontal or slightly off-center medium close-ups for important explanation.
- Use medium shots when gestures or offer handling matter.
- Use close-ups and overheads for material, label, damage, process, interface, or comparison evidence.
- Use wide shots only to establish a necessary social or environmental context.
- Default to static or subtly breathing camera.
- Reserve handheld motion for immediacy and simple tracking, pans, tilts, rotations, or zooms for motivated emphasis.
- Preserve central and lower-middle mobile safe zones for captions.

## Edit Rhythm

- Create the viewer question within `0-3s`.
- Use `1-3s` evidence inserts for most clauses.
- Hold an anchor for `3-6s` when a complete statement needs it.
- Cut on claims, examples, comparison changes, emotional turns, and proof reveals.
- Prefer `anchor -> evidence -> anchor` or `anchor -> evidence sequence -> anchor`.
- Slow slightly for a decision, guarantee, or CTA when useful.

## Offer Entry

Delay offer emphasis until the problem and reason are understandable. Motivate entry through the established question:

- inspect it
- compare it
- touch, stretch, apply, open, configure, read, or use it
- place it into the routine already shown
- reveal it as the answer to the visual mechanism

Follow the interaction with an approved proof trace and a human or system result.

## Speech, Text, And Sound

- Use direct questions, costly consequences, familiar problems, or supported contradictions.
- Explain with plain causal language: problem -> reason -> consequence -> response.
- Keep the voice composed and specific; use controlled humor only when it clarifies.
- Let captions mirror the spoken phrase or isolate one key evidence label.
- Allow one reading task per beat.
- Keep speech foregrounded over a restrained music bed or relevant room tone.
- Use literal sound effects only when they reinforce a visible action.

## Claim Discipline

Separate:

- `approved`: supported by user-supplied facts or owned evidence
- `needs evidence`: plausible but not yet substantiated
- `prohibited`: contradicted, disallowed, or outside the offer's approved scope

Never turn `needs evidence` into spoken copy, captions, visual implication, or authority behavior.
