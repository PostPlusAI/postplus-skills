## Use When

Use when the creator can correct a believable mistaken assumption, explain one practical mechanism, demonstrate it in ordinary use, and end in a changed human state.

Choose another route when:

- the personal verdict matters more than the corrected belief: use `creator recommendation`
- the main value is an input-action-output process: use `problem and solution`
- a matched before/after state is the primary proof: use `transformation`
- the claim cannot be supported by an observable action, detail, or state

## Core Contract

Build one causal chain:

`mistaken belief or lived frustration -> hidden consequence -> corrected understanding -> product mechanism -> observable use proof -> relief or practical confidence`

Lock one belief, one mechanism, one main promise, and one changed state. Do not stack unrelated benefits.

Make the creator sound like a person sharing a useful correction, not a spokesperson reciting a feature list. Let the product earn entry after the viewer understands why the old assumption is incomplete.

## Timing And Visual Rhythm

Use this default rhythm for a 15-30 second ad:

| beat | default duration | visual job | speech job |
| --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| misconception hook | 0-2s | centered close or medium close direct address, or the problem already occurring | state the old belief, frustration, or viewer question |
| reframe | 2-6s | hold the creator or cut to the hidden consequence | explain what the real problem is |
| mechanism and product entry | 5-10s | product enters through handling, fitting, applying, wearing, or another next action | name the practical reason the product helps |
| proof sequence | 8-20s | two to four inserts, normally 1-5s each | narrate only the feature or result currently visible |
| changed state | final 3-6s | return to the creator or hold on the resolved use state | express relief, confidence, habit adoption, or advice |

Treat the ranges as elastic. Compress beats instead of speeding up speech.

Prefer these edit patterns:

- `A-roll -> proof -> proof -> A-roll` for the clearest testimonial arc
- `A-roll -> proof -> A-roll -> proof -> A-roll` when the mechanism needs explanation between actions
- `problem action -> A-roll reframe -> proof -> resolved action` when the visual problem is stronger than a spoken hook

Use direct cuts or motivated jump cuts. Keep the anchor camera static or lightly handheld. Create energy through hands, body, product, liquid, fabric, pets, walking, cleaning, or another proof action before adding camera movement.

## Dialogue Structure

Write five functional sentence units. A unit may span more than one shot, but each unit must perform one job.

1. **Belief:** State the old assumption or frustration in first person.
2. **Reframe:** Contrast the obvious problem with the hidden consequence.
3. **Mechanism:** Explain one reason the product changes that consequence.
4. **Proof narration:** Describe only what the current insert visibly demonstrates.
5. **Verdict:** Close with a changed routine, emotional relief, practical advice, or restrained endorsement.

Useful sentence shapes include:

- `I used to think [common belief], but [contradicting experience].`
- `The problem is not really [obvious issue]; it is [hidden consequence].`
- `[Product action or feature] helps because [plain-language mechanism].`
- `You can see [observable behavior or result].`
- `Now [routine or emotional state is different].`

Use the functions, not the exact wording. Avoid repeating source dialogue or turning every script into an "I used to think" clone.

Target approximately 2.3-2.8 spoken words per second. As a starting range, use 35-55 words for 15-20 seconds and 50-75 words for 20-30 seconds. Reduce the word count when actions, reactions, or practical sounds need room.

## Product Entry

Introduce the product after the reframe is understandable, normally 20-45% into the runtime. Let it enter as the next physical action:

- put it on
- fasten or adjust it
- apply or consume it
- use its relevant control or feature
- place it into the problem environment

Do not pause the story for a detached package presentation. An early product appearance is acceptable when the product itself creates the opening question, but delay its explanation until the reframe or mechanism beat.

## Proof Rules

Match each spoken clause to a visible carrier:

| claim job | acceptable visual carrier |
| --- | --- |
| easier or faster handling | complete the relevant action in readable time |
| fit, stability, or support | show fastening, body contact, weight shift, walking, or movement |
| visibility | show the product and subject separated clearly from the environment |
| reduced mess or effort | show the original friction, prevention action, and cleaner resolved state |
| comfort or confidence | show behavior that supports the verdict; do not treat a smile alone as performance proof |
| transformation | show a credible matched before, process, and after or switch to the `transformation` route |

Require causal closure:

`named consequence -> relevant product action -> observable result -> changed human state`

Treat personal testimony as testimony. Do not present generated scenes, creator enthusiasm, or an unverified before image as independent scientific, medical, or comparative proof.

## Camera, Sound, And Text

- Use centered close or medium-close framing for direct address.
- Use close inserts for hands, fit, controls, contact points, or product behavior.
- Widen only when gait, full-body movement, environment, or the resolved routine is the proof.
- Keep foreground speech clear over restrained supporting music.
- Use practical sounds when they clarify fastening, movement, pouring, cleaning, opening, or another action.
- Add final subtitles in post from approved copy. Break captions into short spoken phrases and verify translation, spelling, labels, and claim language.

## Close

End with one of these functions:

- changed habit: the product now stays in the routine
- practical advice: tell the viewer what detail to check or reconsider
- relief: name the friction that no longer dominates the experience
- reluctant endorsement: acknowledge prior skepticism, then give a measured verdict

Avoid adding urgency, discounts, scarcity, or a purchase command unless the brief explicitly requires a direct-response close. Do not let a hard CTA erase the creator's believable posture.

## Continuity And Generator Risks

Lock creator identity, voice, wardrobe, product geometry, product label, product category, environment, action order, and product state.

Check carefully:

- label-to-script and product-category consistency
- hands, fastening, swallowing, footwear, pet clothing, and body-product contact
- water, wet fur, cloth, liquid, reflections, and surface-state behavior
- face, voice, wardrobe, room, pet, and product continuity across A-roll and proof inserts
- speech density, lip sync, subtitle timing, and bilingual translation
- unsupported before/after evidence or generated testimony presented as a real customer account

Keep logos, small package text, legal copy, prices, reviews, and numerical claims for controlled post-production treatment.

## Avoid

- opening with brand or feature exposition before the belief or pressure is legible
- correcting a trivial belief that does not change product relevance
- naming a mechanism without showing the corresponding product behavior
- beauty shots that do not support the current spoken clause
- proof inserts that show a different product, state, subject, or environment
- generic excitement as the final evidence
- copying a reference creator, dialogue, pet, room, wardrobe, packaging, subtitle design, or shot sequence

## Review Gate

For an existing video, rate each item `Strong / Partial / Weak / Missing` with timecodes:

- mistaken belief or lived-pressure hook
- meaningful reframe
- earned product entry
- plain-language mechanism
- clause-matched observable proof
- causal closure
- changed human state
- natural verdict or advice close

Classify the asset as:

- `complete`: the corrected belief, product action, proof, and changed state form one causal chain
- `structurally complete but weakly proven`: the story resolves, but the visible evidence does not support the spoken promise
- `incomplete`: the product or verdict arrives without a meaningful reframe, mechanism, or proof
