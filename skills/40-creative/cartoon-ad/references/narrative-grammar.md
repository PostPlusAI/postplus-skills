# Narrative Grammar

Use this reference to select the story engine and define the `Cartoon Grammar Plan`.

## Core Principle

Make the advertising argument visible:

`specific pressure -> visible consequence -> offer interaction -> mechanism trace -> proof -> desired human state`

Animation may exaggerate presentation, not product facts or claim strength.

## Narrative Engines

Choose one primary engine. Add a secondary engine only when it removes explanation.

### Pressure Personification

Turn an invisible problem into an original character or agent that interrupts the user's world.

Use for recurring stress, distraction, odor, friction, clutter, or interruption. Avoid copying a reference creature or creating body horror that overwhelms the offer.

### Invisible State Visualization

Represent an unseen state with particles, color, light, gauges, object behavior, or a simple spatial rule.

Use for scent, energy, focus, heat, hydration, signal, noise, or similar properties. Define the code once and reuse it consistently.

### Category Expose

Visualize category friction through quantities, labels, prices, factories, ingredients, repeated objects, or process contrast.

Use only with documented comparisons or category evidence. Treat every comparison as a claim.

### Founder Origin

Build from a supplied personal problem through search, experimentation, and creation to the offer.

Compress real process into readable actions. Do not invent founder history or generic heritage scenes.

### Routine Transformation

Repeat the same routine, location, or social situation before and after offer interaction.

Use when changed behavior, timing, comfort, relationship response, or task completion is the strongest proof. Keep geography and camera family stable.

### Case Character

Follow a representative professional, operator, customer, or user through a specific contradiction and accumulated evidence.

Use for B2B, software, consulting, education, communities, and professional services.

Preferred chain:

`high-value contradiction -> physical evidence of pressure -> failed prior solutions -> offer interaction -> changed protagonist behavior`

Do not replace the protagonist's result with unrelated successful people. Aspirational peers may preview the solution, but are not proof unless they are approved customers or testimonials.

## State Arc

Make four states distinguishable:

1. `baseline`: who the person is before pressure peaks.
2. `pressure`: visible friction, consequence, or loss of control.
3. `transition`: offer entry, interaction, and mechanism trace.
4. `result`: changed posture, behavior, environment, task, or social response.

Do not reduce the result to a smile, glow, or unrelated success montage. Show what the established person can now do, tolerate, finish, enjoy, delegate, understand, or avoid.

## Camera And Edit Grammar

- Favor static or slowly moving medium shots and close-ups for readability.
- Use wide or high-angle frames when environment, system, quantity, or process is the subject.
- Reserve rapid push-ins, impacts, bursts, or speed changes for one planned climax.
- Use 1-3 second beats for hooks and progression, 3-5 seconds for interaction or explanation, and 3-6 seconds for proof or CTA.
- Use hard cuts by default. Use repeated composition or match cuts to clarify causality.
- Keep one dominant subject and one dominant idea per frame.

## Visual Medium

Choose attributes that serve the audience and offer:

- smooth stylized 3D
- tactile knit, felt, clay, paper, or miniature materials
- graphic 2D/3D hybrid
- stylized characters with controlled realistic offer or UI inserts
- object-led animation without a human protagonist

Do not equate cartoon with childish design. Avoid named-style imitation.
