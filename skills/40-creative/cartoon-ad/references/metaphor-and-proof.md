## Pressure Carrier

Make the problem observable through one or more related carriers:

- object: alarm, wallet, calendar, label, receipt, meter, unfinished task
- environment: queue, factory, clinic, crowded room, noisy bedroom, cluttered desk
- body state: posture, breathing, shaking, fatigue, interrupted sleep
- social reaction: partner wakes, colleague waits, customer hesitates
- animated agent: an original personification of pressure

Show the carrier before the offer appears.

## Visual Argument Modes

Choose one primary mode:

### Metaphor

Map an invisible source state to a visible sign and change rule:

`source state -> visual sign -> change after offer interaction`

Keep color, behavior, and scale consistent. Do not let magical effects imply unsupported literal mechanisms.

### Evidence Prop

Accumulate related physical evidence such as time, documents, unused objects, repeated payments, clutter, or failed tools.

Make every prop advance the same argument. Avoid random sadness montage.

### Process

Show how the category, ingredient, service, workflow, or system operates through a readable sequence.

Use only approved process facts. Simplify without changing causality.

### Repeated Routine

Repeat a location, task, or social situation before and after offer interaction.

Lock composition and geography so the changed state reads immediately.

## Offer Type And Interaction

Choose an interaction appropriate to the offer:

- `physical_product`: open, wear, apply, pour, taste, attach, set, carry
- `software`: enter, tap, configure, review output, complete a task
- `service`: join, ask, receive feedback, review a decision, implement advice
- `community`: post a question, join a call, receive peer responses, adopt a practice
- `content`: watch, read, save, learn, apply a method

Show orientation, contact or interface state, and the immediate response. A laptop, phone, meeting, or product merely appearing is `offer entry`, not `offer interaction`.

## Mechanism Trace

Use the lightest trace that explains capability:

- direct physical operation
- before/after object or task state
- simplified UI or workflow
- ingredient or process sequence
- particle or light-state transition
- personified mechanism
- visual equation
- advice received and visibly implemented

Prefer direct operation over metaphor when direct operation is already clear.

## Proof Plan

Classify every result image:

- `solution_preview`: illustrates a possible desirable state but proves nothing
- `behavioral_proof`: the established user performs a previously difficult action
- `functional_proof`: the offer visibly operates or changes an object/task state
- `social_proof`: an approved customer, testimonial, review, or sourced reaction
- `sensory_proof`: texture, sound, vapor, application, or material response
- `process_proof`: approved ingredients, manufacture, testing, coaching, or workflow
- `numeric_proof`: supplied quantity, duration, price, result, or guarantee

For each selected proof, state:

- `result_owner`: `protagonist | approved_customer | testimonial | illustrative_peer`
- `proof_status`: `approved | illustrative | missing`

Illustrative peers and generic happy groups are solution previews, not proof. Use at least two approved proof layers when available.

## Claim Boundaries

- Treat medical, physiological, diagnostic, ingredient, comparative, performance, environmental, price, review, and guarantee statements as claims.
- Use only supplied and approved wording.
- Mark metaphors and unsourced outcomes as illustrative.
- Keep legal copy and dense evidence text out of model-facing prompts.
- Add final numbers, labels, package text, reviews, UI, and subtitles in post.
- Do not show a result that materially exceeds the approved claim.

## Text And Sound

- Use VO to carry the argument and visuals to carry causality.
- Use subtitles or keywords only when they clarify a beat.
- Keep one evidence label or number on screen at a time.
- Contrast pressure and solution through synchronized sound.
- Give every prominent effect a visible source.
